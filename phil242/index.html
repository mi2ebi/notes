ethics misconception 1: ethics is simplistic

nb: this is a claim and this is reality

"ethics is just a dogmatic set of rules: don't lie, don't steal, ..."

ethics is very context sensitive. there may be cases where eg lying is not only permissible but the right thing to do

ethical principles are meant to hold for everyone, insofar as one is intending to be ethical. this doesn't mean everyone is morally required to do the same thing in similar or identical circumstances

ought implies can – what's morally correct for you to do depends on your capabilities. if it's impossible for an agent to perform an action, then said agent is not morally obligated to perform said action

ethical conflict of duties

in ww2 the duty to protect innocent life far outweighed the duty to not lie when the gestapo knocked on your door and asked if you were harboring any jews

there are also cases where there is no clear indication that one duty trumps another, in which case you must just coose what to do

ethics misconception 2: ethical claims are subjective

"ethics is just a matter of opinion" (ethical subjectivism)

ethical claims are objective. why do people think otherwise?

  1. it makes life easier. if ethical claims are subjective there's nothing to argue about

  2. often it's difficult or impossible to determine what is true in ethical analysis

    but this is also true of quantum physics. (eg is the universe deterministic or not? and the EPR paradox) do we think advanced theoretical physics is subjective?

matter of fact / matter of opinion

matter of opinion (subjective claim)
when 2 people disagree both are correct
matter of fact (objective claim)
when 2 people disagree at least one person is incorrect
the claim is capable of being false!
matter of fact matter of opinion ethical claim
2 + 2 = 4 broccoli tastes awful torturing babies for fun is wrong
the coronavirus doesnt exist chocolate ice cream tastes better than vanilla its perfectly fine to kill people if they smell bad
the temperature on venus is 470°F purple is prettier than red raping for personal pleasure is morally good

now obviously these ethical claims are matters of fact

if you take ethical subjectivism seriously, that claim reduces to absurdity

reductio ad absurdum / indirect proof / proof by contradiction
assume the opposite of what you want to prove, show it leads to an absurdity, therefore what you want to prove is true
  1. assume ethical subjectivism is true
  2. then it's morally good for me to kill a student in order to take their nice computer. outside the hypothetical this is false
  3. therefore ethical subjectivism is false

ethics misconception 3: cultural relativism is 'the way to go'

"ethics is purely a function of culture." cultures differ with respect to what they call moral, so no one culture can adjudicate right and wrong

cultural relativism is a false and dangerous theory

comes from the observations of 20th century anthropologists looking at (problematic, non-neutral) 19th century anthropological studies

anthropology
a descriptive, neutral account of the beliefs and practices of a culture
not judging said culture from the perspective of one's own

cultural relativism argument
(p1) cultures differ with respect to what they call moral
(c) what is moral varies by culture ↔ there are no universal ethical truths

but "there are no universal ethical truths" is a universal ethical truth! so cultural relativism is incoherent

what if there were a culture that endorses, say, tyrannizing other cultures, because they have an ethical belief that says cultural relativism is false? by the cultural relativists' own admission they have to respect that

crash course in argument analysis:

inductive argument
the conclusion only follows from the premises with some degree of probability
deductive argument
the conclusion follows from the premises with strict necessity (i.e. the premises force it, and no other conclusion could follow)
valid argument
when assuming the premises are true, the conclusion is forced

the cultural relativist argument is presented as deductive

(p1) if it rains you will get wet
(p2) it rained
(c) you will get wet

this↑ argument is valid

(p1) if it rains you will get wet
(p2) you will get wet
(c) it rained

this↑ argument is invalid. you could get wet for other reasons. maybe you fell into a pool

is the cultural relativism argument valid?

no! it's like saying

(p1) cultures differ with respect to what they think earth's shape is
(c) """there is no universal shape of the earth"""

and do you see how fucking ridiculous that is. why are people tempted to do that for ethics?

two confusions:

  1. confusing epistemological ~ metaphysical claims
  2. confusing descriptive ~ prescriptive claims
prescriptive claim
a claim about how things ought to be / should be

(p1) is an epistemological claim ("what they call moral"), and (c) is a metaphysical one ("what is moral"). likewise (p1) is descriptive and (c) is prescriptive

example 2. in some cultures it's viewed as immoral for women to feel pleasure during sex, so to prevent that from happening they surgically remove some or all of their vulva. (this is something that still happens today.) again the cultural relativist would say that female genital mutilation is moral for the cultures that practice it, and again this is absurd

what would a cultural relativist have to say about a person born in nazi germany, who thinks the eugenics policy is morally wrong and wants to resist? that that person is morally wrong
